Increased intracranial pressure: more neuroanatomy

The skull (once fontanelles close) is a rigid cavity. The total volume must remain constant. Therefore, if one of the three intracranial components (brain, blood, CSF) increases, one or the two other components must decrease. Causes of increased intracranial pressure include space occupying lesions (such as tumors) or hydrocephalus. Hydrocephalus is caused by either obstruction to the CSF flow (obstructive hydrocephalus) or increased CSF production (non-obstructive hydrocephalus). Initially, the intracranial components exhibit some compliance and are able to compensate for increases in volume without major increases in intracranial pressure (for example, by decreasing the amount of blood or CSF intracranially). However, after a certain point, minor increases in volume lead to abrupt increases in intracranial pressure. At that point, there is risk of brain herniation.

Typical signs or symptoms of increased intracranial pressure include vomiting, limitation of vertical movement (especially upgaze), unsteadiness, headache (especially if increases with straining and on squatting and wakes the patient up from sleep), and, of course, altered mental status. What happens before the fontanelles close? Any of the above can happen, but also there is another mechanism to tolerate the increase in intracranial pressure: increase in head size.

Macrocephaly can be a sign of increased intracranial pressure in an infant, especially when the increase in head circumference is subacute over weeks and the patient crosses several percentile lines in their growth curve. In these situations there are typically other signs and symptoms of increased intracranial pressure as described above. In contrast, when the patient smoothly follows a percentile line in their head circumference growth chart and there are no other concerning signs or symptoms, the patient most likely has familial macrocephaly or prominent subarachnoid spaces.

Putting it all together

Q8. Another attending tells you that apart from structural, infectious, and autoimmune causes of altered mental status, there are plenty of mechanisms by which your mental status can be altered. Can you think of some of these causes?










There are many many causes of altered mental status in pediatrics. How to approach such a vast topic?

First of all, keep the patient alive providing cardiorespiratory and support of all physiological functions as needed.

As always in medicine, a good history and physical exam is crucial to rule in or out the most important potential etiologies.

A simple but effective approach is presented in this chapter (you can even download the pdf).

The first crucial aspect is to define the depth of altered mental status, which can go from mild alteration of behavior to very severe coma or even death by neurologic criteria. The second crucial aspect to define is to what potential etiologies has this patient been exposed to, such as history of infections, risk factors, social exposure to potential toxics. The third crucial aspect is to determine the symptoms that preceded or accompanied the altered mental status.

These simple aspects may further direct history and physical examination.

A good mnemonic to think about other potential etiologies is AEIOU TIPS (alcohol, encephalopathy, insulin, opiates, uremia, trauma, infection, poisoning, and seizure).
