Sensorimotor deficits and demyelinating disorders







Sensorimotor deficits

The spinothalamic tract: carries crude touch, temperature, and pain:
First neuron: Dorsal sensory ganglion
Second neuron: Gray matter of spinal cord
Third neuron: Ventral posterior lateral nucleus of the thalamus



The posterior column-medial lemniscal pathway carries fine touch, vibration, and propioception:
First neuron: Dorsal sensory ganglion
Second neuron: Nucleus gracilis (lower extremity) and nucleus cuneatus (upper extremity)
Third neuron: Ventral posterior lateral nucleus of the thalamus



The sensation of the face is carried by two different systems:

1.The trigeminothalamic tract for crude touch, temperature, and pain (equivalent to the spinothalamic system for the body): First neuron: Mostly in the trigeminal ganglion (also sensory ganglia in VII, IX, and X for a portion of the ear and tympanus)
Second neuron: Spinal nucleus of the trigeminus
Third neuron: Ventral posterior medial nucleus of the thalamus

1.The trigeminal lemnicus for fine touch, vibration, and propioception (equivalent to the dorsal column-medial lemniscal pathway for the body): First neuron: Mostly in the trigeminal ganglion (also sensory ganglia in VII, IX, and X for a portion of the ear and tympanus)
Second neuron: Chief (pontine) sensory nucleus of the trigeminus
Third neuron: Ventral posterior medial nucleus of the thalamus

The corticospinal tract and the corticobulbar tract carry the motor orders from the primary motor cortex (first neuron) to the lower motor neuron (second neuron) in the ventral horn of the spinal cord (corticospinal tract) or the motor nuclei of the brainstem (corticobulbar tract). The decussation of the pyramids occurs in the spinomedullary junction with the fibers for the upper extremity crossing rostral (before) than the fibers for the lower extremity.

Other sensorimotor tracts carry mainly unconscious sensorimotor information. On the sensory side, the main ones are the spinocerebellar tracts. On the motor side, the main ones are the rubrospinal tract, which activates flexor muscles and inhibits extensor muscles and ends in the cervical spine and the vestibulospinal tracts, which favors extension for maintenance of posture.

Demyelinating disorders

Demyelinating disorders are immune-mediated processes that attack the central nervous system causing damage predominantly in the white matter. The most common demyelinating disorders are multiple sclerosis, neuromyelitis optica, and MOG-associated disease.



The most classical demyelinating disease is multiple sclerosis. The diagnosis of multiple sclerosis has always been based on demonstration of white matter lesions that are disseminated in space and disseminated in time. Although these broad categories remain the cornerstone on how you diagnose multiple sclerosis, the specific diagnostic criteria with which you demonstrate dissemination in space and dissemination in time have varied over the years.

SUMMARY

Demyelinating diseases include multiple sclerosis, neuromyelitis optica, and MOG-associated disease.

Simplistically, you can think of neuromyelitis optica as similar to multiple sclerosis, but with more severe attacks, bigger or longer lesions, and with far less resolution after acute attacks. Similarly, you can think of MOG-associated disease as similar to multiple sclerosis, but with fluffier, bigger, or longer lesions, and generally monophasic (a single attack). Obviously, this is an oversimplification, but it may help conceptualize these diseases if you are exposed to them for the first time.

Demyelinating diseases cause focal lesions in the white matter, which provide interesting presentations for learning neuroanatomy.
