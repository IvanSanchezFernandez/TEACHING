Memory 3

The patient presented with difficulty finding her way even in familiar surroundings. She also had forgetfulness of recent events. The symptoms progressed for a month until she was disoriented everywhere except in the immediate neighborhood of her own home and could not recall events that occurred several minutes before.

Normal physical exam and neurological exam.

Formal neuropsychological evaluation showed topographical disorientation, amnesia (retrograde for 2 years and anterograde), and inattention.

Brain MRI showed T2 hyperintensity in bilateral hippocampi, the right retrosplenial region, and the right inferior precuneus as shown in Figure 1 here.

Normal EEG.

Normal CSF.

Positive anti-Hu antibody in serum.

Negative neoplastic work-up.

Patient died within a year. Truly fascinating case report.

SUMMARY

Limbic encephalitis in children is rare, very rare. A study from 12 hospitals in 4 European countries for 3 years found a total of 10 patients with pediatric limbic encephalitis. As study on anti-Hu antibodies from the centralized laboratory in France for 11 years found 243 cases in adults and 8 cases in children.";

In children, only a minority of limbic encephalitis are paraneoplastic. However, a neoplastic screening should be conducted.

Viruses like HHV6 can potentially cause a direct acute infection of the limbic area, an acute parainfectious immune reaction, or a slow progressive infection/inflammation process and it is not clear which one(s) is the cause for HHV6 limbic encephalitis. Maybe a combination of these mechanisms.

The clinical presentation of limbic encephalitis can be very unusual very unusual.

Perseveration, inattention, executive function, planning, and visuospatial functions can be easily tested at bedside.
