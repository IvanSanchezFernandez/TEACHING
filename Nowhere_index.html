The underutilization

In the USA there are approximately 330 million people.

In the USA there are approximately 3 million adults and 0.5 million pediatric patients with epilepsy.

In the USA there are approximately 1 million people with refractory epilepsy. Among those, approximately 100,000 to 500,000 patients with refractory epilepsy are surgical candidates, but only 2,000 epilepsy surgeries are done per year.

In the rest of the world things are not better and probably are worse. In a Canadian study fewer than 2% of patients who may have benefited from epilepsy surgery received it.







How is this possible?

There are widespread misconceptions around epilepsy surgery among patients, families, and physicians (even among neurologists and pediatric neurologists). The Dunning-Kruger effect may explain part of this situation, but not the whole situation.

How on Earth is this possible?

Let's introduce the TOTREP. The TOTREP always acts rationally choosing the actions that most increase their utility. Neoclassical economics is based on the idea that people are rational in making choices to optimize their outcomes when presented with perfect information.









An important characteristics of the TOTREP is that it does not exist. There is strong empirical evidence since the 1970s that human decision making is biased and irrational (authors won the 2002 Nobel prize in economics for this work). The fact that humans do not behave rationally making choices to optimize their outcomes when presented with perfect information led to the creation of the field of behavioral economics, which analyzes how humans behave empirically rather than how they theoretically behave based on some idealized way.





How all of this is relevant to epilepsy surgery?





Availability bias

A bias by which the probability of an event is estimated by the ease which instances of that event can be brought to mind. The easier an event can be brought to mind, the higher its estimated probability. For example, what are more common in English, words with "r" as the first letter or words with "r" as the third letter?

It is easy to remember a patient whose stroke rapidly improved (at least in the short term) after treatment in the hospital. However, it is less dramatic and less salient to see the improvement in seizure frequency (which occurs at home, not in the hospital). Also, it is easy to retrieve from memory the 1% case of epilepsy surgery who had a bad outcome (in the hospital) than the 99% who had a very good outcome.

And English words with "r" as third letter are also more frequent than English words with "r" as a first letter, even if probably you are still convinced that that is not the case because of the availability bias.





Anchoring

A bias by which the estimation of a probability is based on an initial point (based on prior knowledge) which is moderately adjusted based on new data to yield the final value. For example, if you hear in a group website that a certain type of wine costs $120 dollars per bottle, probably you would expect to pay somewhere between $100 and $140 per bottle, but not to pay $30 per bottle.

Similarly, you have been anchored to believe that tPA is an extremely effective therapy and epilepsy surgery is dangerous. You might adjust your anchors when you receive more realistic information, but probably not enough to see the full difference between these therapies (see table above).





Prospect theory

Yes, an economic theory which won the Nobel prize is very relevant to decision making in medicine. Outcomes that are very probable (let's say 95%) are much underweighted when compared with outcomes that are certain (100%). This leads to risk aversion in choices involving gains and risk seeking in choices involving losses.

You are likely to buy coffee in a shop that offers a $1 discount on a $5 coffee if you pay by credit card. You are not likely to buy coffee in a shop that charges $1 extra on a $4 coffee if you pay cash...because you are loss averse even if the options are absolutely the same.

Similarly, the stroke patient is in a situation where a loss is clear and immediate (immediate death or disability) so the patient (and team) is risk-seeking (accepts risky treatments) to avoid the loss. In contrast, a patient with refractory epilepsy perceives the current situation as stable and epilepsy surgery as a gain diluted in the future (improving seizure control) not as an avoidance of immediate loss, and is, therefore, risk averse (avoids surgery because it entails risks).

In fact, long-term follow-up of patients with refractory epilepsy shows that periods of prolonged seizure freedom are frequent, but just temporary: in a study of 128 children with refractory epilepsy followed for a median of 12 years, 56% of patients had at least a remission of over 1 year, but it was temporary.





Statistical life versus identified life

A statistical life is a life improved or saved by a policy change that modifies probabilities a little across a large group. An identified life is a life improved or saved by an intervention in a particular individual. Charities do not present data, just present a poster-child.

Similarly, the effect of interventions on stroke patients are easy to identify on an individual basis, while the effect of epilepsy surgery is statistical (for example, a huge reduction in the risk of SUDEP).





Drive behavior change









The idea that humans are rational decision makers which choose the best option when presented with perfect information is an empirically disproven idea. That is why you will not see commercials or public health campaigns like the ones in the videos. They just do not work and do not change behavior.

In 2003 the American Academy of Neurology and the American Epilepsy Society released a practice parameter informing about the delays in referring to epilepsy surgery and the need for early referral for epilepsy surgery evaluation. The response was no change whatsoever.





The field of stroke was able to disseminate simple, actionable data to doctors and to the general population. This is extremely important because it anchors and nudges the default behavior (if you have a stroke you have to go to the hospital and if you qualify, you should get tPA).





In contrast, the field of epilepsy has provided complex guidance that is not actionable.



Have you seen something like this?



Have you seen something like this?



Fortunately, in 2022, the International League Against Epilepsy (ILAE) provided a clear, unambigous official statement: "Referral for a surgical evaluation should be offered to every patient with drug-resistant epilepsy (up to 70 years of age), as soon as drug resistance is ascertained, regardless of epilepsy duration, sex, socioeconomic status, seizure type, epilepsy type (including epileptic encephalopathies), localization, and comorbidities (including severe psychiatric comorbidity like psychogenic nonepileptic seizures [PNES] or substance abuse) if patients are cooperative with management."

This means: no excuses. As soon as the patient is refractory, evaluation for epilepsy surgery. This also has legal consequences: if that does not happen, it can be considered substandard care.

Hopefully, the ILAE will translate this message into a simple and actionable message to doctors and the general population.









SUMMARY

The next time you see somebody running for a code stroke think this: out of 8 patients who will eventually receive tPA, 1 will avert death or dependence, but there is no life expectancy benefit beyond 1 year.

The next time that you see a patient with epilepsy who has failed at least 2 appropriately chosen and dosed antiseizure medications think this: among 3 patients who eventually receive epilepsy surgery, 2 will markedly improve (sustained seizure freedom) and their life expectancy will improve by 5-6 years.

Also, the risk of tPA is higher than the risk of epilepsy surgery.

Most likely, you should be more proactive with patients with refractory epilepsy. Approximately one third of patients with epilepsy have refractory epilepsy. If you are not offering a presurgical evaluation to approximately a third of your patients with epilepsy, you are not following current clinical practice guidelines (legally, you are providing substandard care).
