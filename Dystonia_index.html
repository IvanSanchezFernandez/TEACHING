Dystonia

Now that you know how to identify the main types of movement disorders, let's focus on dystonia.



Q4. What is dystonia?


Loss of tone in a region of the body (for example, the hand)
Repetitive oscillating movements around a joint
Sustained contraction of agonist and antagonist muscles leading to abnormal twisting postures








Q5. Regarding the characteristics of dystonia, check all correct answers below:


The sensory trick consists of a predisposition and particular ability in patients with dystonia to perform magic tricks (guessing the right card from a deck, pull a rabbit out of a hat, etc.)
Overflow in patients with dystonia consists of their abnormal movements extending from one region to other regions of the body
Dystonia was considered to be a psychogenic disorder by prominent doctors in the late XIX century
Overflow in patients with dystonia consists of emotional overwhelming and crying with relatively minor stimuli
Dystonia can occur during one action, but not at all during a different action by the same muscles (for example, writing causes dystonia, but playing an instrument does not)








Dystonias can affect the lower body, the upper body or the whole body. These high-quality videos are supplementary material in the excellent article on dystonia caused by the KMT2B gene.



There are a few dystonias that affect children which are worth knowing about.

Q9. By now your fame in movement disorders has crossed borders. A doctor from another country sends you a child who started having jerks at the age of 5 years. He occasionaly has dystonic posturing. The symptoms affect mainly the upper body and he has a relatively normal gait and lower extremity function. While you examine him, you notice that he has to close the door three times (not two or four, always has to be three) when he enters a room. Family history is incomplete because parents were polysubstance users and abandoned him in an orphanage. Your diagnosis is:


DYT11
Sequelae from her rough childhood
Structural brain lesion from a likely abnormal pregnancy and delivery
Dopa-responsive dystonia








What about treatment? Treatment of dystonia is symptomatic in most patients.

First treatment: L-dopa/carbidopa. Always worth a try. If it is dopa-responsive dystonia you are going to diagnose and cure the disease. If not, many other forms of dystonia partially respond to L-dopa. Always try.

Symptomatic treatments: Trihexyphenidyl. Baclofen. Botulinum toxin.

Other less established symptomatic treatments: Clonazepam. Carbamazepine. Dopamine antagonists. Dopamine depletors.

Neurosurgery: For example, deep brain stimulation of the pallidus interna. When the surgical candidate is well chosen and the therapy works, it can be life-changing.





SUMMARY

After reading this post, you have reviewed the main concepts in dystonia.



You can classify and name the main symptoms in movement disorders. Importantly, you have a visual idea of how they look like.



Dystonia is a sustained contraction of agonist and antagonist muscles leading to abnormal twisting postures. You now know that dystonia can have unusual features which made doctors in the past believe it was of psychogenic origin: sensory trick, overflow, and task-specific symptoms.



The main dystonias you should be aware of in children are: DYT1 (Jewish ancestry, evolution from one foot to whole body within 5 years), DYT5 (evolution from one foot to the whole body, but diurnal variation with worse symptoms at night), and DYT11 (upper body involvement, myoclonus more prominent than dystonia, psychiatric history). All three of DYT1, DYT5, and DYT11 are autosomal dominant.



Treatment of pediatric dystonia should always include a trial of L-dopa (DYT5 responds to it and others might respond partially). Symptomatic treatment includes trihexyphenidyl, baclofen, and botulinum toxin. In well-selected patients, neurosurgery can be life-changing.
