Epilepsy for developmental and behavioral pediatricians

In this post you will learn the main features of epilepsy and how it affects development and behavior.





Seizures do not necessarily mean epilepsy.



Q1. A 14 year-old adolescent comes to your clinic because, just for fun, he likes to run toward the wall and hit his head hard against the wall. Each time he hits his head on the wall he has a seizure. Seizures have never occurred out of that context. The next best step in avoiding new seizures is:


Start seizure medications. He is having seizures, therefore, he needs an antiseizure medication
Stop hitting his head against the wall. These are provoked seizures and they will stop after the abnormal stimulus is stopped. No antiseizure medication is needed








The key concept here is that provoked seizures occur in a normal brain, but unprovoked seizures occur in an abnormal brain, a brain with a predisposition to have more seizures. Remember, even if your brain is completely normal, if you hit your head hard enough against the wall you will have a seizure, if you take enough cocaine, you will have a seizure, and so on. You do not need antiseizure medications, you should just stop being exposed to these abnormal stimuli and your seizures will go away (obviously, if you do those things long enough your brain may be so abnormal that you may develop unprovoked seizures, but for now we are only considering the situation where the insults occur in the short term and you only have provoked seizures).

What is epilepsy then? Epilepsy (loosely defined) is a disease of the brain in which there is a chronic predisposition to have unprovoked seizures. Of course, the not loosely defined version is slightly more complicated. The current official definition of epilepsy is here.

Q4. Regarding the definition of epilepsy, check all correct answers:


If you have one unprovoked seizure and a structural brain lesion that causes epilepsy in most patients, you have epilepsy
If you have one unprovoked seizure and an EEG with epileptiform activity typically seen in the type of unprovoked seizure you had, you have epilepsy
If you have many provoked seizures and an EEG with epileptiform activity you have epilepsy
In rare cases, you can have epilepsy without seizures because you have an epilepsy syndrome in which you never had a seizure
You need an abnormal EEG to diagnose epilepsy








As a general rule, you only use long-term treatment with antiseizure medication for epilepsy.

Taking a seizure history takes time. In general, it is best to just describe how was the event. If there is a video of the event that is even better.



Q6. Regarding seizure classification, check all correct answers below:


You should always believe that seizures classified as 'generalized tonic-clonic' are generalized tonic-clonic, especially when there is no description of the event
Patients tend not to describe the seizure: ask them questions to describe the seizure (describe the situation when it happened, step by step movements, eyes open or close, how it started, how it progressed, one side more affected than the other)
The classification of seizures and epileptic syndromes is fixed and does not change
You should be very suspicious of the accuracy of a seizure classification in a clinical history where the seizure is classified but not described
Epilepsy is a clinical diagnosis and taking an appropriate clinical history is crucial








Always describe the seizure in detail. If you want, you can also try to classify it using the most recent classification of seizure types and epilepsy syndromes, but that is not crucial if you are not an epileptologist.



Once you understood these very simple concepts, the evaluation of any type of seizures becomes straightforward: if you have a patient with staring spells, take a detailed description of the events (when they happen, how long they last, triggers if any, ability to respond during the event, etc.). With that information, refer to pediatric neurology. They will decide what is the next step (if any).



What if your patient has other types of seizures?: take a detailed description of the events (when they happen, how long they last, triggers if any, ability to respond during the event, description of the movements if any, etc.). With that information, refer to pediatric neurology. They will decide what is the next step (if any).

Can epilepsies cause developmental regression? Glad you asked! Seizures, antiseizure medications, and epileptiform discharges can cause cognitive problems.

Frequent seizures make it difficult for the child to learn (both because of missing school and because of the impact of seizures on the brain).

Phenobarbital causes some impairment in all aspects of cognition. Topiramate causes some language impairment. Levetiracetam frequently causes behavioral dysregulation.

Interictal epileptiform discharges can cause cognitive regression. Continuous spikes and waves during sleep is an epileptic encephalopathy in which there is cognitive regression because epileptiform activity dysrupts sleep. Landau-Kleffner syndrome causes language regression because epileptiform activity causes dysruption of temporal lobe function especially during sleep. Read more about that here. Then, should epileptiform discharges be treated? Well, it is complicated.



Treatment for ADHD, depression, or anxiety should not be modified because the patient has epilepsy or because the patient is on antiseizure medications unless there are specific contraindications. Some SSRIs reduce seizure threshold, but generally a patient with epilepsy will not have more seizures because of that if they take their antiseizure medications. When in doubt, talk to the pediatric neurologist, but in general, you should not change your behavioral medications because the patient has epilepsy or because the patient is on antiseizure medications.



One in three people with epilepsy have at least a psychiatric diagnosis during their lifetime and the presence of these psychiatric problems is a bad prognostic sign. Screen for psychiatric diagnoses in people with epilepsy.





SUMMARY

After reading this post, you have reviewed the main concepts in epilepsy.



Describe the seizure. Describe the seizure. Always describe the seizure. The more detail the better. If there is a video that is ideal. Classifying the seizure is less important than describing the seizure. Take a good history.



Epilepsy is a clinical diagnosis. Take a good history. If you are not a neurologist, you do not have to make the decision to classify an event as a seizure or a clinical presentation as epilepsy, or to decide whether the patient needs antiseizure medications, but you definitively are responsible for taking a good history and obtain a proper description of the events.



Make the world a better place: erradicate 'seizure disorder' and other aberrant terminology from medicine.



EEGs typically help in the field of epilepsy. However, they are not necessary or sufficient to make a diagnosis of epilepsy. Epilepsy is a clinical diagnosis.



Cognitive and behavioral problems are frequent in patients with epilepsy. Epilepsy is frequent in patients with developmental delay. In general, you should not change your behavioral medications because the patient has epilepsy or is on antiseizure medications. However, if you have a concern that the patient is doing worse than before, check with your pediatric neurologist if the antiseizure medications (especially phenobarbital, topiramate, or levetiracetam) are negatively influencing cognition or behavior in your patient.



Epileptiform discharges can cause regression. If you think your patient is regressing, consult the pediatric neurologist. There are many causes of regression and generally none of them is good. A child with regression should always be evaluated by the pediatric neurologist.
