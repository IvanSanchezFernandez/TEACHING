Apart from the etiologies we reviewed in question 2, remember that ECMO (apart from all the benefits it provides) is our stroke production machine: it may facilitate cardiac arrhythmias and cardiac thrombi, it may damage large and small vessels (because of abrupt changes in blood pressure), it may cause both episodes of hypercoagulability, and hypocoagulability, and it might facilitate infections across the whole cardiovascular system/ECMO circuit.
It is not surprising that stroke occurs in approximately 10-60% of pediatric patients on ECMO as shown in this and this study. Variability in the proportion of patients with stroke after ECMO depends on ECMO duration and neuroimaging studies and probably many more patients have subclinical strokes.

Perinatal stroke is a devastating condition associated with serious sequelae in most patients. After a perinatal stroke, expected outcomes are:
  cerebral palsy/motor deficits: 30-50%
  cognitive deficits: 50-60%
  epilepsy: 15-55%

Stroke in non-neonates has somewhat better outcomes, but not much better with only approximately one third having no deficits and approximately two thirds having a variable combination of motor, cognitive, and behavioral deficits.







Clinical cases



Ready to treat some patients? Calculate the Pediatric NIHSS and, if you think the patient has a stroke apply an appropriate therapy. Easy, right?



Supportive management of stroke includes:
  Normal O2, normal CO2, normal pH
  Normothermia
  Normovolemia
  Euglycemia with goal 70-120 mg/dl
  Normal blood pressure with SBP goal 50th to 90th percentile for age
  Anticoagulation only if cardioembolic origin or cervicocephalic arterial dissection is suspected

Apart from that, the management of pediatric stroke is not fully established yet

SUMMARY

After reading this post, you have reviewed the main concepts in pediatric stroke.



Epidemiology:
Pediatric stroke has an incidence of approximately 4-6/100,000, which is similar to the incidence of rolandic epilepsy. Pediatric stroke in non-neonates is slightly less frequent with an incidence of 2-4/100,000 (still approximately the same incidence as rolandic epilepsy). In contrast, perinatal stroke has a much higher incidence of 29-50/100,000, which is slightly higher than the incidence of stroke in adults 20-44 years of age and approximately 2-4 times lower than the incidence of stroke in adults 45-64 years of age. You can remember it as neonates having the incidence of stroke of roughly 50 year-old adults.



Etiology:
Causes of pediatric stroke are heterogeneous and can be classified by origin:
Heart: Endocarditis. Congenital heart disease. Valvulopathy. Arrhythmia. Cardiomyopathy.
Blood vessels: Vasculitis. Post-varicella arteriopathy. Moyamoya disease. Focal arteriopathy of childhood. Takayasu arteritis.
Blood/coagulation disorders: Antiphospholipid antibodies. Sickle cell disease. Inherited prothrombotic and lipid abnormalities. Lymphoproliferative disorders.
Other: Trauma. Infection. Cancer. Toxins. Surgery. Metabolic disorders (hyperhomocysteinemia, congenital disorders of glycosilation).
ECMO combines multiple potential mechanisms for causing stroke including thrombi from heart and large vessels, fluctuations in blood pressure, iatrogenic coagulation disorders, and the potential for disection, infection, and surgery. Therefore, 10-60% of pediatric patients on ECMO are found to have a stroke.



Types of stroke:
Among ischemic stroke, arterial ischemic stroke represents approximately 80% and cerebral sinovenous thrombosis approximately 20% of cases.
Among hemorrhagic stroke, intracerebral stroke is 2-3 times more frequent than subarachnoid hemorrhage.



Clinical presentation:
Stroke in children presents with the typical symptoms you expect in a stroke (hemiplegia, sensory loss, aphasia, hemianopia), but also with symptoms which may be difficult to attribute to a stroke (dystonia, headache, seizures). A particularly difficult situation occurs with the patient presenting with first time seizure with post-ictal paresis (and probably with altered mental status): is there a stroke as an etiology? Fortunately, stroke is a rare cause of new-onset seizures in children and typically there are other focal neurological signs.
Keep stroke in your differential diagnosis for acute/subacute presentations, but stroke is rare in non-neonate children without risk factors.



Outcome:
Pediatric stroke is associated with serious sequelae including motor deficits, epilepsy, cognitive deficits, and behavioral disorders. Only a minority of children with pediatric stroke (one third or less) remain with no serious sequelae. Remember that when informing families.



Treatment:
How to manage pediatric stroke is an area of active research. The relative rarity of pediatric stroke and the marked delays to diagnosis (even in large pediatric stroke centers) makes any study challenging. Although evidence is limited, if children are diagnosed before 4.5 hours, thrombolisis might be a reasonable option in children at least 2 years of age with PedNIHSS>=4 and <=24, with proven infarct and arterial occlusion on neuroimaging and no contraindications. Arterial thrombectomy is an option for children with large vessel occlussion. For patients with stroke in the context of sickle cell disease, exchange transfusion is a very reasonable treatment.
